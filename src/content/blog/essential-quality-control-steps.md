In the garment industry, hope is not a strategy. Quality must be engineered into the production process at every stage, not just caught at the end. For global brands sourcing in South Asia, a robust Quality Control (QC) framework is the only way to prevent costly returns and brand damage.

### The Four Pillars of QC

A professional sourcing team implements four distinct "Gateways" for quality:

1.  **Fabric & Trim Inspection (The Source)**: Checking raw materials for color fastness, shrinkage, and weight (GSM) before a single cut is made.
2.  **Pre-Production Meeting (The Blueprint)**: A final alignment where the tech pack, approved sample, and production plan are reviewed with the factory's line managers.
3.  **In-Line Inspection (The Process)**: Catching mistakes while the machines are running. If a stitch is wrong on the first 10 garments, it can be fixed. If it's wrong on 1,000, it's a disaster.
4.  **Final Random Inspection (The Verdict)**: Using the **AQL (Acceptable Quality Level)** standard to statistically verify the entire batch before it is packed.

All quality begins with the [Technical Pack](/insights/tech-pack-efficiency-guide). If your documentation is flawed, your QC will fail regardless of factory competence.

### Understanding AQL 2.5
Most high-end apparel brands use the AQL 2.5 standard. This means that in a batch of 1,000 garments, a specific number of pieces are randomly inspected. If the number of "Major" defects exceeds the threshold (based on ISO tables), the entire shipment is rejected for 100% sorting.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Director's Take**: "The most common mistake is skipping the In-Line inspection. By the time you do a Final Inspection, the garments are already packed. Catching a fault at the needle point saves weeks of rework."

### Common Defects to Watch For
- **Shading**: Variation in color between different panels of the same garment.
- **Wavy Seams**: Poor tension in the sewing lines, causing puckering.
- **Broken Stitches**: Structural failures that compromise the garment's longevity.
- **Measurement Deviations**: Failure to adhere to the tolerance levels defined in the tech pack.

#### QC FAQ
**Q: Can a shipment pass AQL and still have some defects?**
A: Yes. AQL is a statistical probability. It ensures the percentage of defects is below a commercially acceptable limit, but it doesn't guarantee 0% defects (which is why 100% inspection is sometimes used for luxury goods).

**Q: Who pays for the rework if a batch fails inspection?**
A: The factory is responsible for rework costs. However, the brand often pays the "time cost" of the delay, which is why preventive QC is so vital.
