For a fashion startup, the manufacturing partner isn't just a vendor; they are the foundation of the brand. Yet, many small labels rush this process, falling into expensive traps that could have been avoided with professional vetting.

### 1. Prioritizing Price Over Compliance
The most dangerous mistake is choosing a factory solely because they offered the lowest quote. In 2026, an uncompliant factory is a massive liability. If a supplier is operating without ESG certifications or safety standards, a single audit failure can lead to your entire stock being blocked or your brand being publicly shamed.

### 2. Misaligning Capacity (Small Fish, Big Pond)
Startups often aim for the "Best Factory in Dhaka"—the one that produces for Zara or H&M. However, if your order is only 500 units and the factory's minimum is 10,000, you will be deprioritized. Your order will be pushed back for "Big Client" production, leading to massive delays and poor communication. Look for a factory where your business **matters**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Director's Take**: "Match your scale. A medium-sized, high-quality factory will give a startup significantly better attention and service than a massive conglomerate where you're just 0.1% of their turnover."

### 3. Ignoring the Tech Pack
Factories are manufacturers, not designers. If you send a loose sketch and "hope for the best," the factory will make assumptions. When the sample arrives looking nothing like your vision, you'll spend weeks in "Email Ping-Pong." A professional Tech Pack is the only way to ensure the factory is following your exact specifications.

### 4. Failing to Visit (or Use a Local Representative)
A glossy website and a friendly salesperson are easy to manufacture. The reality of the factory floor—its cleanliness, the morale of the workers, the maintenance of the machines—can only be verified in person. Use our [10 Must-Ask Questions for Your Sourcing Agent](/insights/sourcing-agent-checklist-10-questions) to prepare for your vetting visits.

### 5. No Signed Quality Agreement
Many startups assume the factory will "just make it good." Without a signed Quality Manual that defines AQL (Acceptable Quality Levels) and defect classifications, you have no legal or commercial recourse if the shipment arrives with faults.

#### Selection FAQ
**Q: What is the ideal order size for a startup in Bangladesh?**
A: While many large factories require 3,000+ units, there is a growing ecosystem of high-quality "Boutique" factories that cater to orders as low as 300–500 units per style.

**Q: Do I need to pay for samples?**
A: Yes, usually. Reliable factories invest time and material into sampling. Expect to pay 2x-3x the production price for development samples.
