In global sourcing, "On Time" is often a matter of interpretation unless it's strictly defined by a structured alignment process. For fashion brands, a delay of just two weeks can mean the difference between a successful seasonal launch and an inventory of deadstock.

### The Source of Delay: Information Asymmetry
Most production delays aren't caused by factory incompetence, but by **misalignment**. This occurs when the brand assumes the factory knows a specific detail, and the factory assumes the brand has already approved a certain stage.

### Three Pillars of Supplier Alignment

To eliminate these gaps, Sinha Sourcing Hub utilizes a triple-pillar alignment framework:

1.  **The Master Production Calendar (MPC)**: A shared, living document that tracks every milestone—from yarn booking to final QC. Both the brand and the factory must sign off on this weekly.
2.  **Clear Communication Protocols**: Establishing a "Single Point of Truth." All approvals must happen through a centralized system (like a PLM or a dedicated project thread), not scattered across WhatsApp and personal emails.
3.  **The Commercial Alignment Contract**: Contracts should include realistic "Grace Periods" and predefined "Delay Penalties" (Force Majeure clauses) that protect both parties while incentivizing performance.

> [!NOTE]
> **Director's Take**: "A contract isn't a weapon; it's a map. It defines exactly what happens when things go wrong, ensuring that problem-solving takes priority over finger-pointing."

### Effective Communication Cadence
- **Pre-Production**: Daily check-ins until the first stitch is made.
- **Mid-Production**: Twice-weekly status reports with photographic evidence.
- **Post-Production**: A "Lessons Learned" meeting to optimize the next round.

### Alignment FAQ
**Q: How do I handle unexpected delays like port strikes?**
A: This is where "Risk Diversification" comes in. Your alignment strategy should include a contingency carrier or an alternative port of exit defined *before* the disaster hits.

**Q: Should I pay early to incentivize speed?**
A: Usually, no. Adhering to the agreed-upon payment milestones (e.g., 30% deposit, 70% at shipment) keeps the incentive structure balanced.
